# Egged Bus Line 390

## Executive Summary
Egged bus line 390 is an intercity route operated by Egged — Israel's largest public bus company — running along the southern coastal corridor connecting Tel Aviv and Ashkelon. It sits within a historically significant network of coastal bus routes that have played a central role in Israeli public life, civilian mobility, and national security events since the mid-20th century.

## History and Background of Egged and Line 390

Egged (Hebrew: אֶגֶד, meaning "Union") was founded in 1933 through a merger of four smaller intercity bus cooperatives in and around Tel Aviv, with its name bestowed by the poet Hayim Nahman Bialik ([Egged – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egged_(company))). Over subsequent decades, Egged absorbed multiple regional carriers — including the Northern *Shahar* company and Southern *Drom Yehuda* company in 1951, and Jerusalem's *Hamekasher* company in 1961 — growing into a nationwide intercity transport network that became the backbone of Israeli public transportation. At its peak, Egged operated a fleet of roughly 2,950 buses, carried around 900,000 passengers per day, and provided approximately 35% of Israel's total public transport services.

Bus line 390 operated as an intercity route along Israel's southern coastal corridor, running between Tel Aviv and Ashkelon. Like other major Egged intercity lines in the 300–400 numbering range, it served as an important commuter and travel link, connecting coastal cities and enabling workforce mobility particularly in an era when private car ownership was limited and intercity rail was sparse. ## The Southern Coastal Corridor and Its National Significance

Egged's intercity lines along the Tel Aviv–southern coast corridor have historically been central to Israeli social and economic life. The route 390 corridor — like its closely related neighbor route 300 (also Tel Aviv to Ashkelon) — served towns, kibbutzim, and moshavim along the coastal plain, functioning as an essential artery for everyday civilian movement. In 2019, Egged converted from a long-standing worker cooperative model to a standard company structure, reflecting broader deregulation trends that had already introduced competition from operators such as Dan, Kavim, and Superbus, gradually reducing Egged's near-monopoly on intercity travel ([Egged – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egged_(company))).

The significance of these coastal bus routes extends beyond transportation. They represent a layer of Israeli civic infrastructure that absorbed the country's rapid demographic growth, mass immigration waves, and the demands of a dispersed population living amid ongoing security challenges. Routes along this corridor have periodically intersected with major national security events, highlighting how civilian transit and political reality are deeply intertwined in Israel.

## Notable Incidents on Related Coastal Routes

The most historically prominent incident linked to an Egged route on this same southern coastal corridor was the **Bus 300 Affair** of April 12–13, 1984, involving Egged route 300 (Tel Aviv to Ashkelon) — a route closely related to the 390 corridor ([Bus 300 affair – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_300_affair)). Four armed Palestinians from Gaza boarded the bus in Ashdod, hijacked it, and attempted to drive it toward the Egyptian border. Israeli Sayeret Matkal commandos stormed the vehicle; two hijackers were killed in the assault and two were captured alive but subsequently executed by Shin Bet operatives. The resulting scandal triggered a major political and judicial crisis, led to the resignation of the Shin Bet chief, and prompted the establishment of the Landau Commission to investigate the conduct of the security services.
